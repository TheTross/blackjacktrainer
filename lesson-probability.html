Probability & Variance

This lesson explains how often different outcomes happen in blackjack, why the house edge is small but real, and why short‑term streaks can look nothing like the long‑run averages.

Win Rate • House Edge • Variance

Big Picture Numbers

In a typical multi‑deck game with reasonable rules and solid basic strategy, the player wins a little over four out of every ten hands, the dealer wins a little under five out of ten, and the rest push.

  • The exact percentages depend on rules like number of decks, whether the dealer hits soft 17, and the blackjack payout.
  • With good rules and basic strategy, the house edge is usually well under 1% of total money bet.
  • Without basic strategy, common mistakes can push that edge several times higher.

These numbers come from computer simulations of huge samples of hands, not from what happens in a single session at a live table.

What “Expectation” Means

Expected value (EV) is the long‑run average result per unit bet if you could replay the same situation thousands of times under the same rules.

  • For any specific combination of your hand and the dealer’s upcard, each action (hit, stand, double, split) has its own EV.
  • Basic strategy charts simply pick the action with the highest EV in that situation, even if that action still loses money on average.
  • Following basic strategy lowers the house edge as far as the rules allow, but does not flip it in your favour.
A decision can be correct in EV terms and still lose on this particular hand. Probability describes tendencies over many hands, not guarantees for the next one.

Variance & Streaks

Variance measures how widely actual results swing around that long‑run expectation. In blackjack, variance is what produces the familiar waves of hot streaks and cold stretches.

  • Even with perfect basic strategy, you will sometimes see long runs of losing hands.
  • Winning streaks are just as real: you can easily have nights where you run far above the theoretical edge.
  • The “long run” where the math clearly shows through can mean hundreds of thousands or millions of hands, not a weekend trip.

Understanding variance helps separate emotion from math: a rough downswing does not mean the rules or strategy suddenly stopped working.

Connecting Numbers to the Trainer

The interactive trainer on the main page uses a fixed rule set and a shuffled deck to generate situations. When it suggests an action, that suggestion is based on which choice tends to have the best EV in similar spots.

  • Each hand you see in the trainer is one random sample from the same probability model described here.
  • Sometimes the recommended play will win, sometimes it will lose, but over many hands it will do better than the alternatives.
  • Your “accuracy” in the trainer measures how often you match that recommended decision, not how much you would have won or lost in money terms.
When the trainer suggests a move that feels uncomfortable, pause and ask: “If I repeated this exact spot thousands of times, which choice gives me the best long‑run average?”

Practical Takeaways

Probability and variance are not there to make blackjack mysterious. They simply describe how often different outcomes occur and how bumpy the ride will feel along the way.

  • Use a basic strategy chart as your default for every hand instead of “going with your gut.”
  • Expect both winning streaks and losing streaks and plan your bankroll so that either outcome is manageable.
  • Judge your decisions by whether they follow sound strategy, not by what happened on one or two hands.
