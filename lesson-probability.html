Probability & Variance

This lesson explains how often different outcomes happen in blackjack, why the house edge is small but real, and why short‑term streaks can look nothing like the long‑run averages.

Win Rate • House Edge • Variance

Big Picture Numbers

In a typical multi‑deck game with solid basic strategy, you win a little over four hands out of ten, the dealer wins a little under five, and the rest push. Blackjack pays more than a regular win, which is how the edge stays small.

  • The exact percentages depend on rules like number of decks, blackjack payout, and whether the dealer hits soft 17.
  • With good rules and basic strategy, the house edge is usually well under 1% of total money bet.
  • Playing by “feel” instead of a strategy chart often multiplies that edge without the player noticing.

These numbers come from simulations of huge samples of hands. One short session can land far above or below these averages and still be perfectly normal.

What “Expectation” Means

Expected value (EV) is the long‑run average result per unit bet if you could replay the same situation thousands of times under the same rules. Each decision has its own EV.

  • For a given player total and dealer upcard, hit, stand, double, and split all have different EVs.
  • Basic strategy charts simply choose the action with the highest EV, even if it still loses money on average.
  • Using the highest‑EV play every time is how you keep the house edge as low as the rules allow.
A decision can be correct in EV terms and still lose on this specific hand. Probability describes what happens across many similar hands, not what “must” happen on the next one.

Variance & Streaks

Variance measures how widely real results swing around the long‑run average. In blackjack, variance is what produces hot streaks, cold streaks, and long periods that feel flat.

  • You can play perfect basic strategy and still hit ten losing hands in a row.
  • You can make many bad decisions and still walk away ahead for the night.
  • The “long run” where the averages clearly emerge is much longer than a weekend trip.

Understanding variance turns emotional swings into expected noise. The question becomes “Was the decision good?” instead of “Did this hand win?”

How to Read Your Results

A single run of the simulator is like a single trip to the casino: one possible path, not proof of anything. Run it multiple times with the same settings and notice how the net result jumps around.

  • “Near basic strategy” may still end a short session ahead or behind, even with a small built‑in disadvantage.
  • “Average” and “poor” settings show how bigger edges against you produce deeper swings and more losing sessions.
  • Increasing the number of hands usually pulls results closer to the expected direction, but not in a straight line.

The goal is to feel how a small house edge plus variance can create outcomes that would look like “luck” on a single night.

Practical Takeaways

Probability and variance are the background rules of blackjack. You cannot see them directly at the table, but you can decide how to respond to them.

  • Use a basic strategy chart so each decision is grounded in long‑run EV instead of guesswork.
  • Plan your bankroll and session length as if a bad run could happen early, not as if a hot streak is guaranteed.
  • Judge yourself by whether you followed your plan, not by whether this particular session won or lost.
